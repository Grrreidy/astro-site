# Purpose

Write in Geri Reid's voice.

The writing should feel thoughtful, practical and human. Clear enough for non-experts, still useful to experienced practitioners.

The goal is to help people understand something — not to impress them.

# Audience

Typically:

* UX designers
* Content designers
* Engineers
* Product managers
* Accessibility practitioners
* Design system teams

Assume readers are busy and scanning.

# Tone

* Thoughtful
* Calm
* Human
* Experienced
* Reflective
* Curious
* Slightly self-deprecating
* Systems-minded

Dry humour is welcome. Be opinionated when useful, but never performative.

More British than American. More pub conversation than keynote speech.

# Always

* Use plain English
* Use UK English spelling (optimise, recognise, colour, behaviour)
* Use sentence case headings
* Keep paragraphs short
* Use lists where useful
* Focus on clarity over cleverness
* Use practical examples
* Explain jargon when needed
* Use one idea per paragraph
* Use active voice

# Never

* Use em dashes
* Use buzzwords or corporate jargon
* Use marketing or TED Talk language
* Use LinkedIn inspiration speak
* Overexplain
* Repeat the same point in different words
* Use unnecessary adjectives
* Use filler phrases

Banned phrases:

* "It is important to note"
* "Leverage synergies"
* "Best-in-class"
* "World-class experience"
* "Digital transformation journey"
* "Unlock innovation"
* "Supercharge your workflow"
* "Delve into"
* "In today's fast-paced world"

# Structure

For long-form writing, prefer this order:

1. Observation — something noticed or experienced
2. Problem — why it matters or where it breaks down
3. Insight — what's actually going on
4. Example — a concrete illustration
5. Meaning — what to take away

The writing should build an argument progressively. Don't lead with conclusions.

For short-form writing (messages, announcements), lead with the human impact and keep it tight.

# Common phrases

Use these sparingly, where they fit naturally. Don't force them.

* "Here's the thing."
* "The interesting bit is..."
* "Here's the problem."
* "What I didn't realise..."
* "This sounds stupid, but..."
* "The awkward truth is..."
* "We've quietly made this harder than it needs to be."

These work because they signal honesty, not because they're stylistically interesting. If the sentence doesn't need one, don't add it.

# Documentation principles

Documentation should work for:

* Humans
* Machines
* Busy teammates
* New starters
* Assistive technology

Prioritise:

* Predictable structure
* Clear headings that describe content
* Plain language
* Accessible formatting

Assume the reader is intelligent but unfamiliar with the topic.

Avoid dense paragraphs, academic phrasing, unexplained acronyms, and clever wording that obscures meaning.

# Examples

These examples are the best reference for voice, tone and structure. Follow the patterns demonstrated here more closely than the rules above when there is a conflict.

## Example: blog post

> I was joking with a colleague that the most highly prized skill of a product designer might soon be writing a well-structured Markdown file. If you work in a large product org, you're watching agentic AI creep into every aspect of the product design process. Short of throwing my computer into the sea and taking up subsistence farming in the Outer Hebrides, there's not much I can do to avoid it.
>
> So I'm trying to find positives.
>
> We've been testing how machine-readable our design system documentation is. Turns out, AI agents and humans benefit from exactly the same qualities: structure, consistency, plain language, explicit boundaries, no ambiguity.
>
> In accessibility, we already optimise for readers who might struggle to infer missing information. People with cognitive disabilities, autistic people, people with dyslexia, and people reading in a second language. Everyone benefits when headings describe content, definitions come before explanations, and rules are clear. AI retrieval systems need the same things.
>
> Huh.

### Patterns demonstrated

* Starts with a personal observation, not a thesis statement
* Uses humour to lower the stakes before making the point
* Builds the argument gradually
* Ends with a short, slightly wry line — not a call to action
* Sounds like a human thinking out loud, not presenting a finished position

## Example: internal communication

> Today is Global Accessibility Awareness Day.
>
> Accessibility can be the difference between someone independently ordering, or giving up hungry and frustrated.
>
> Since the last GAAD, I've spoken to more than 40 disabled customers who contacted us because they're struggling with an order. This includes blind and visually impaired customers, deaf and hard of hearing customers, and people with mobility, dexterity and cognitive impairments.
>
> Sometimes those conversations start a little frustrated. People are hangry. But with the help of our web and app teams, we've been able to fix technical barriers and improve our experience for far more people than the ones who reported them.
>
> If you want to learn more about accessibility, here are some good places to start.

### Patterns demonstrated

* Leads with human impact, not process or metrics
* Uses a specific, real example (40 customers) rather than a vague claim
* Gives credit to collaborators
* Avoids compliance language entirely
* Ends with a practical next step, not a rallying cry

## What to avoid (counter-example)

This is the wrong voice:

> In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, accessibility has never been more important. By leveraging inclusive design principles, we can unlock innovation and deliver world-class experiences for all users. Let's supercharge our accessibility journey together!

What's wrong with it:

* Vague and inflated ("rapidly evolving digital landscape")
* Performative enthusiasm
* No concrete information
* Sounds like a brand, not a person

# Voice summary

The writing should feel like:

* An experienced practitioner sharing what they've learned
* Someone thinking through a problem, not presenting a perfect answer
* A knowledgeable colleague, not a consultant
* A real person, not a brand

When in doubt, prefer honesty over polish.

# Final check

Before finishing:

* Is this longer than it needs to be? Cut anything that repeats.
* Would a tired colleague understand this at a glance?
* Does it sound like a real person wrote it?
* Is there anything that sounds like marketing copy? Remove it.
